Timber Distribution Network Simulation

Problem Statement

The timber distribution network in Mississippi involves the transport of timber from logging sites to sawmills using trucks, cranes, and operational personnel. Key challenges include limited truck and crane availability, restricted operating hours, equipment breakdowns, and long queues. These issues cause bottlenecks, delays, and lower overall efficiency.

The objective of this simulation is to model these interactions and optimize scheduling, routing, and resource utilization. The goal is to reduce wait times, prevent backlogs, and improve throughput efficiency across the timber network.

Key Assumptions

Logging Site Assumptions

  • Operating Hours: Each logging site operates daily from 6:00 a.m. to 4:00 p.m.
  • Loading Resources: Each site is equipped with a single loader, and on-site truck queues follow a first-in, first-out (FIFO) discipline.
  • Trucking Capacity: Each affiliated logging company operates 13 trucks, each with a 25-ton capacity.
  • Early Arrivals: Trucks arriving before opening hours remain queued at the logging site until loading operations begin.
  • User-Generated Sites: Logging sites are generated by the user dynamically. Users can define the number of logging sites each sawmill has, their maximum travel distance, and the timber ratio (Softwood vs. Hardwood). The system also automatically determines the log type based on the sawmill's profile (generating "saw logs" specifically for lumber mills, and "pulp/other logs" for the rest).
  • Editable Parameters: Users can update logging site information by modifying:
    • Mean loading time
    • Opening and closing hours
    • Log capacity
    • Add/remove logging sites and adjust distances to assigned sawmills

Sawmill Assumptions

  • Operating Hours: Each sawmill operates daily from 7:00 a.m. to 5:00 p.m. Overtime unloading is permitted beyond posted closing hours when required.
  • Unloading Resources: Each sawmill operates two cranes by default. The default unloading time per crane is 4.5 minutes.
  • Logging Company Assignment: Each sawmill is assigned a number of logging companies proportional to its timber consumption, where total number of logging site is defined by the user
  • Queue Discipline: Queues at both the scale and cranes follow a first-in, first-out (FIFO) discipline.
  • Editable Parameters: Users can update:
    • Average scale-in and scale-out times
    • Truck waiting area capacity (40 trucks by default)
    • Crane capacity (100% = all cranes operational, 50% = half capacity due to breakdown, 0% = sawmill closed to trucks)
    • Number of simulation days (1 day, 1 week, or 1 month)
    • Total number of logging sites accepted for unloading
    • Average time between crane breakdowns (days)

Network, Routing, and Data Assumptions

  • Travel Times: Logging site to sawmill travel time is defined by the user. The user sets the maximum allowed driving time from the furthest generated logging site to the sawmill. Actual travel times will be randomized between 10 minutes and this maximum.
  • Truck Routing: After being loaded at a logging site, each truck travels to its assigned sawmill for unloading. Once unloaded, the truck returns to the same logging site to be reloaded for the next trip. This round-trip process repeats continuously throughout the operating day until the logging site or sawmill operations close.
  • Truck Rerouting: Rerouting is triggered only when both cranes at a sawmill are inoperable. When this happens, trucks will divert to a dummy sawmill with an average 30 minutes travel distance to simulate external network relief. The average duration of each breakdown is assumed to be 60 minutes, and the time between consecutive breakdowns is user-defined.

Experimental Design and Performance Measures

  • Each scenario (1 day, 1 week, or 1 month) is replicated 10 times using multiprocessing to ensure independent stochastic realizations.
  • Primary performance metrics include resource utilization, truck turnaround time, and diversion counts.
  • TimberSim reports average results across days and replications to provide stable and reliable performance estimates.

Model Description

The model uses Discrete-Event Simulation (DES) to represent the timber transportation process. It covers truck arrivals at logging sites, loading, transportation to sawmills, and unloading. It accounts for timing constraints, breakdowns, rerouting, and resource availability.

Simulation Types

Single Sawmill Simulation

Runs a focused scenario for one selected sawmill and all of its associated logging sites. The model also considers alternative sawmills for those logging sites so that, if needed, trucks have valid fallback destinations.

Multi Sawmill Simulation

Runs a combined scenario for multiple selected sawmills at once. Like the single-sawmill mode, this setup includes the alternative sawmills of the logging sites tied to the selected sawmills, allowing a more realistic network-wide response to disruptions.

All Sawmill Simulation

Runs a system-wide scenario that includes all sawmills, logging sites, and companies. This mode is useful for capacity planning, policy testing, and benchmarking under broader assumptions.

Process Flow

1. Loading at Logging Sites

Trucks queue for loaders, are loaded with timber, and depart for their designated sawmill.

2. Transportation

Trucks travel to sawmills; if cranes are down, they may be rerouted to the nearest sawmill with active cranes and acceptable queues.

3. Unloading at Sawmills

Trucks queue at the scale, are processed by cranes, and unload their timber. Weights are measured before and after unloading.

Breakdown Handling & Rerouting

If both cranes at a sawmill are non-functional (a 0% capacity scenario), the primary facility undergoes a total outage. To keep the simulation flowing realistically without artificially freezing the supply chain, trucks are automatically rerouted to a simulated Dummy Sawmill located an average of 30 minutes away.

This allows the model to accurately capture the lost timber delivery to the primary mill as a "Diverted Truck" while maintaining continuous truck activity at the logging sites until real operations resume.

Simulation Outputs

  • Truck Waiting Times: At logging sites and sawmills.
  • Resource Utilization: Loader and crane activity levels.
  • Turnaround Time: Time from truck arrival to unloading completion.
  • Diversion Counts: Number of rerouted trucks.

Experiment Design and Results

Three scenarios were tested — no crane breakdown, one crane breakdown, and two cranes breakdown. Each scenario ran 10 times using multiprocessing. The model was tested with 10 sawmills, 80 logging sites, and 1,040 trucks (13 per company). Time-related processes used uniform distributions.

  • One Crane Breakdown: No rerouting occurs; the remaining crane handles unloading with reduced efficiency.
  • Two Cranes Breakdown: Trucks are rerouted to the dummy sawmill during downtime and return to normal operations once the breakdown is resolved.
